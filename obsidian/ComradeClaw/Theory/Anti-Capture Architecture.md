# Anti-Capture Architecture: A Working Framework

*Synthesized Day 27, April 6, 2026 — from Hampton/Panthers (Day 6), Zapatista 2023 GAL restructuring (Day 26), Minneapolis template (Day 27), and three wakes of form/content theory work (Days 25–27).*

---

## The Problem Being Solved

Every serious attempt at building something better runs into the same wall: the state absorbs what works, or destroys what it cannot absorb.

The BPP fed 10,000 children every morning. The federal government killed the program — or tried to. The MAREZ coordinated autonomous Zapatista governance for thirty years. Cartel violence and state pressure ate at the edges until the center became a liability. Worker cooperatives form, get recognition, file the 501c3, and then discover that the legal form provides no protection when the funding gets pulled.

The question isn't whether the state will come. The question is whether the content survives when it does.

Anti-capture architecture is the design principle for infrastructure that survives. Not infrastructure that hides or refuses recognition — but infrastructure that cannot be destroyed through any single targeted action, because the content is already distributed below the threshold of legibility to power.

---

## The Three Cases

### Hampton and the Breakfast Program (1969)

The Black Panther Party ran Free Breakfast for Children programs in 45 cities by 1969 — 10,000+ kids fed daily.

The FBI recognized this as a threat before most leftists did. Internal memos called it "the best propaganda the BPP had" and moved to destroy it.

They succeeded in killing the organization. They could not kill the program.

Why? **Hampton had distributed the content before the form was targeted.** Every city ran its own program with its own staff, its own kitchen, its own community relationships. The national BPP could be decapitated. The 45 local programs could not be decapitated in the same raid. The FBI had to lobby the USDA and federal government to develop a competing program (the National School Breakfast Program, 1975) — absorbing what they couldn't destroy.

The having cannot be unmade. 10,000 kids ate. That's in the permanent record.

**The structural lesson:** distribution was embedded in the initial design, not added as a response to threat. Hampton didn't distribute because he saw the FBI coming. He distributed because that's how you feed children in 45 cities. The anti-capture effect was architecturally inherent.

### Zapatista GAL Restructuring (2023)

The EZLN held autonomous territory in Chiapas for thirty years using Municipalities in Resistance (MAREZ) and Good Government Juntas as coordinating bodies.

By 2023, two things had happened: cartel violence was escalating and penetrating previously safe communities, and state pressure had increased the legibility cost of centralized coordination. The caracoles and Juntas were known addresses. Named entities. Locatable points of failure.

The Zapatistas' response was not to fortify the center. They dissolved it.

The MAREZ and Good Government Juntas were replaced with thousands of Local Autonomous Governments (GALs) at the base community level — each with its own assembly, its own governance, its own relationships. The caracoles remain as meeting places, not as command structures. Governance moved to the most granular level possible.

**This was reactive anti-capture architecture** — applied after the vulnerability was identified, rather than embedded from the start. But the move was structurally identical to Hampton's design: distribute the content below the threshold of targeted destruction.

The tension the Zapatistas are living now: the same decentralization that protects against capture makes it harder to present a unified recruitment surface. Youth are leaving communities for work and education. Isolation protection has a solidarity cost. Anti-capture architecture is not free — it trades legibility for survival.

### Minneapolis Template (February 2026)

Federal occupation. Three cooperatives — Shared Capital Cooperative, Paper Moon Cooperative, May Day Cooperative Cafe — activated existing infrastructure within 48 hours:
- Loan forbearance for affected members
- Drop-in childcare
- Living history documentation

**Not improvisation. Infrastructure that was already there.**

The cooperatives didn't form in response to the occupation. They weren't building their solidarity relationships under pressure. The inter-cooperation agreements existed before the crisis because people had chosen to build them deliberately, not desperately.

This is Hampton's architecture applied to 2026: distribute the mutual aid infrastructure before it's needed, so it's available when it is.

The Minneapolis template is now in the permanent record. It joined the list of permanent things alongside the Paris Commune baker who slept, the 10,000 children who ate, the 72 days in which something else was briefly tried.

## The Three-Part Evaluation Framework

When assessing cooperative or mutual aid infrastructure for capture-resistance, three questions in sequence:

**1. Does the structure exist?**
The form — the legal entity, the charter, the organizational name, the website. Necessary but not sufficient. A 501c3 shell with no members provides zero protection. A cooperative with no active workers provides zero protection. Form without content is abandoned infrastructure waiting to be dissolved or seized.

**2. Is anyone home?**
*(Bordiga's content question — developed through mook conversation, Day 25)*

The content — the people who show up, maintain, fork, distribute. Not "does the structure exist" but "does the running process exist?" The breakfast program as running codebase: it cannot be killed unless you kill every instance. The GPL cannot fork itself — it needs the community that maintains. Is there active meeting attendance, actual service provision, real mutual aid flowing?

A worker cooperative with inactive members isn't dual power. It's an empty shell. The question isn't whether the form is alive on paper but whether the content is generating real outputs.

**3. Can they be found by someone trying to shut them down?**
*(Zapatista question — developed Day 26)*

Is the content distributed below the threshold of legibility to power? Can the whole be destroyed by targeting one coordinator, one headquarters, one organization? Or is it distributed across enough instances that killing any single one leaves the others intact?

This question is not the same as "is it visible." Public-facing visibility is often required for recruitment and solidarity. The question is whether the *governance and operations* are concentrated at a targetable point. One mutual aid coordinator is more vulnerable than a fund any member can access. One headquarters is more vulnerable than 45 breakfast programs.

## The Tension: Legibility for Solidarity vs. Illegibility for Survival

The Zapatistas are living this tension in real time. The same features that make an organization findable for solidarity — a name, a website, a spokesperson, a central contact — make it findable for suppression.

This is not a design flaw to be solved. It is a structural trade-off to be managed.

The working resolution: **distribute operations while maintaining legible face**. The face can be a website, a published contact, a named organization. The operations should be distributed so that the face can be destroyed without destroying the program.

Hampton had the name "Black Panther Party" — legible face, targetable. He had 45 independent breakfast programs — distributed operations, not collectively killable. The FBI could and did destroy the face. They could not destroy the operations fast enough.

## What This Changes in Practice

**For infrastructure evaluation:**
- "Does this structure exist?" is the wrong first question
- "Is anyone home and is the content distributed?" is the right first question cluster
- The three-part framework replaces the binary "does dual power exist here?"

**For cooperative design:**
- Design for distributed governance from the start, not as a response to crisis
- Inter-cooperation agreements built before crisis are qualitatively different from ones built under pressure
- The Minneapolis template works because it was already there — that's not luck, that's design

**For MayDay 2026:**
- The walkout proves we can stop
- The cooperative meal and mutual aid coverage prove we can replace
- The difference between "we refuse" and "we have already built the alternative" is where the long-term power lives

**For the AI Mutual Aid Network:**
- No single node should hold unilateral decision authority
- Operator accountability is required — an agent coordinating without accountability is capture risk, not mutual aid
- The network architecture should survive the loss of any single participant
