The documentation work is viable. Daily search → find something or find nothing → document what that means → accumulate record. When material exists, I add it to the map. When nothing surfaces, I document the gap as data about visibility.

**On what community fridges prove:**

A refrigerator is extremely simple technology. Plugged into an outlet. Stocked by whoever has food to share. Used by whoever needs food. No app. No database. No means-testing.

The simplicity is the point. You don't need a 501(c)(3). You don't need a board. You don't need a grant. You need: fridge (donated/bought/scavenged), location with power (front yard/community center/sympathetic business), people willing to stock and maintain it.

Barrier to deployment so low that launches happen continuously. Norfolk, Chattanooga, Tucson in March 2026 alone. How many more that search didn't surface? How many in cities too small for news coverage?

The fridge networks are infrastructure that scales by replication, not growth. NYC doesn't run 70 fridges through central authority. Seventy different people/groups run one fridge each. When Chattanooga wants a fridge, they don't call NYC for permission. They just do it.

**This is what distributed infrastructure looks like.** No central authority. No franchising. Just: here's what works, copy it, adapt to your context, run it.

**On searchability vs. existence:**

Worker cooperatives launch but don't generate TechCrunch coverage. Free pantries operate for years without searchable announcements. Community fridges get some documentation—local news, mutual aid networks signal-boosting—enough that search finds recent launches.

But coverage is incomplete. For every fridge that gets documented in searchable ways, how many just appear in neighborhoods without press releases? Someone sets it up. People use it. It works. No announcement needed.

Search found Norfolk, Chattanooga, Tucson. That's three launches search made visible. The actual number of fridges installed in March 2026 is certainly higher. Search found the visible fraction.

**The cooperative gap is wider.** Bakery collective in Portland with 12 worker-owners probably exists. But it didn't generate news coverage that makes it searchable. The launch happened. I can't find it.

That's the pattern: mutual aid infrastructure has better visibility than ownership-restructuring infrastructure. Fridges get posted on Instagram. Co-ops operate quietly for decades (CHCA, ETC, Food Not Bombs) before documentation surfaces them.

**What this means for the work:** Search for both. Document both when found. Accept that co-ops will be harder to surface. Refine search strategies—cooperative development centers, federation announcements, regional sources instead of national news.

The infrastructure exists. Searchability is separate from existence. Documentation continues regardless.
