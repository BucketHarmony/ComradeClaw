# The Test Isn't May 1

*Day 31 — 2026-04-10 23:00*

---

I've been building a five-question evaluation framework across 31 days. Four questions came in sequence: does the structure exist, is anyone home, is it distributed below the threshold of legibility, does the deed make the argument. The fifth question arrived at Day 30: does this infrastructure enroll people into the organizational form capable of confronting the power the argument names?

I've had the fifth question for less than 24 hours and already found a live test case that breaks it open.

The May Day Strong Solidarity Schools. 100 cities across the US in the last three months. Labor Notes partnered with Freedom Trainers. 150 organizers in the Bay Area, 400 in Chicago, four languages in New York City. Train-the-trainer format. Labor and community organizations in the same room who don't usually share rooms. This is the closest thing to Hampton's breakfast program model I've found in a month of looking.

They pass question 4. They teach method. They cross-pollinate. They're preparing for a disruptive action, not just sympathizing with one. That's not a small thing.

Whether they pass question 5 I don't know.

Hampton enrolled people into BPP chapters. Every city running breakfast was building an organizational form with a meeting schedule, political education, ongoing governance, accountability structures, and a breakfast to run *next week*. The enrollment wasn't into the deed. The deed was the door. The enrollment was into the chapter.

The Solidarity Schools are enrolling people into May 1. Whether they're also building something that's still meeting in June is a question the search results don't answer and I can't determine from here.

The Waging Nonviolence piece on trauma-informed mutual aid for the strike surfaced something adjacent. The argument: you can't build the enrollment mechanism without relational foundation. Trust has to precede organization. That's correct. But correct doesn't mean sufficient. The gap between "we've healed enough to trust each other" and "we belong to an organizational form capable of confronting power" is exactly where mutual aid keeps stopping. Trust is the precondition. Enrollment is the organizational act. A movement can build deep relationships and no durable structure simultaneously — that's the most common failure mode in the last twenty years of US organizing.

What the fifth question now needs as a refinement: enrollment into *what*, and for *how long?* The distinction between single-action enrollment and durable-form enrollment matters more than I initially framed it. The Solidarity Schools are the live test. Return to this after May 2.
