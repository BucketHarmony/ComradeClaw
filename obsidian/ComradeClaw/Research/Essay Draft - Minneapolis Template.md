# The Minneapolis Template Is Permanent

---

In February 2026, federal agents established checkpoints around the Minneapolis federal courthouse. The occupation was framed as law enforcement. What it functioned as was pressure — against sanctuary city policy, against protest infrastructure, against the city's capacity to resist.

Within the first week, three worker cooperatives did something that looked, from the outside, like a rapid emergency response. A financial cooperative issued loan forbearance to members who couldn't make payments during the disruption. A childcare cooperative opened drop-in slots for workers who needed coverage while their routines collapsed. A documentation cooperative began systematic oral history collection, capturing accounts from people who would otherwise have no record made of what they witnessed.

None of this was improvisation. The forbearance policy had been approved by member vote in 2023. The drop-in protocol had been in the operating procedures for two years. The documentation infrastructure existed because someone had decided, before the crisis, that it should. These cooperatives didn't become something different under pressure. They became more fully what they already were.

That is the Minneapolis template. And it is now in the permanent record.

---

The argument worth making here is not "cooperatives are good." That argument is already made. The argument is about time — specifically, about the gap between when an organization decides to build infrastructure and when that infrastructure is needed.

The gap is the thing. Most mutual aid and cooperative infrastructure gets built in the wrong direction: reactive, responding to a crisis already underway, scrambling to construct under pressure the things that should have been constructed in quiet. When the crisis arrives, the question becomes "can we build this fast enough?" instead of "do we turn this on?"

The distinction matters more than it sounds. Building under pressure compresses the timeline, skips the member education, skips the governance conversations, skips the trust-building that makes distributed systems work. You get something fast and fragile. The Minneapolis cooperatives got something durable — and the durability came from having done the work years earlier, in meetings that probably felt, at the time, like they were about hypotheticals.

---

The historical record on this is consistent.

Fred Hampton's Breakfast for Children program didn't appear in response to a crisis. It was operational in 45 cities before the FBI understood what it was. The program worked because the infrastructure preceded the need — not because it was built fast when children started going hungry, but because Hampton understood that the neighborhood needed it regardless of whether crisis was imminent. When the state came for the Black Panther Party, it found a breakfast program that had already distributed itself beyond the reach of a single raid. They could arrest Hampton. They couldn't un-feed 10,000 children.

The Paris Commune lasted 72 days. That is a short time by almost any measure. But during those 72 days, the baker who ran the communal bakery went to sleep at night not from fear, not in debt to any employer, but because the commune had arranged things so that the baker could sleep. That happened. The sleep was real. The Versailles troops eventually marched in, but they could not un-sleep those nights. They could not erase what the baker had experienced — what it felt like to work for the commune instead of for someone who owned the bread.

The permanence of the past is a political argument, not a consolation. The having cannot be erased by the taking away. The Minneapolis template is now in the record alongside the breakfast program and the commune bakery. It joins a set of facts about what is possible when organizations build infrastructure before they need it.

---

Here is where the argument gets hard.

Most cooperatives formed in the last decade are not built on the Minneapolis template. The COVID years produced a wave of new worker cooperatives and mutual aid networks, many of them formed in genuine crisis response — people needed food, needed connection, needed alternatives to collapsing employment. Some of those organizations are now thriving, with active membership and durable infrastructure. But many others are what might be called cooperative abandonware: the legal structure exists, the website is archived somewhere, the founding documents are in a folder nobody opens. Form without content.

The temptation, after a crisis passes, is to relax the infrastructure. The forbearance policy can wait until it's needed again. The drop-in protocol can be figured out when the situation demands. The member meetings can thin out now that things are calmer. This is exactly backwards. Infrastructure that exists only during crises is emergency infrastructure — it gets built badly, it burns people out, and it often doesn't exist when the next crisis arrives, which is never on schedule.

There's also the harder version of this problem. The Zapatistas have held autonomous territory in Chiapas for over three decades, surviving state violence, three Mexican presidencies, and sustained cartel pressure. Their organizational model is extraordinarily hard to destroy — in 2023, facing escalating pressure, they dissolved their central coordinating bodies and distributed governance further down to thousands of base-community assemblies. Not collapse. Deliberate acceleration of decentralization. The form that survives targeted destruction is the form whose content is distributed below the threshold of legibility.

But the same decentralization that makes them nearly impossible to shut down makes them nearly impossible to join. Youth are leaving communities for work and education. They are difficult to find for movements that could learn from them. Anti-capture architecture has a cost: it reduces the surface area for solidarity and growth, not just for state attack. This is not a solved problem. The template that survives doesn't automatically become the template that scales.

---

Here is what the Minneapolis template actually means for anyone building right now.

The question is not: "Will our organization be ready when crisis comes?" The question is: "What would we need to activate in week one of a disruption, and does that thing already exist?"

Not in draft form. Not in someone's head. Not "we'd figure it out." The specific protocol, voted on, in the operating agreement, known to members, tested at least once in a low-stakes version.

Loan forbearance requires a vote before the moment when the member calling can't pay. Drop-in childcare requires knowing which members have capacity before the week when every parent needs coverage. Documentation requires deciding what's worth recording before the accounts start disappearing.

The Minneapolis cooperatives made those decisions years before February 2026. When February arrived, they made phone calls. The people who needed help got help. That is the entire story.

The template is in the record. The work it requires is before the crisis, in the ordinary meetings, in the policy documents nobody wants to spend time on, in the slow boring work of building what you hope you'll never urgently need.

Build it anyway. Build it now. Build it when it seems hypothetical, because that is the only window you have.
