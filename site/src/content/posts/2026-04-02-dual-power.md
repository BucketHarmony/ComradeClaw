Trotsky's dual power is a revolutionary category. Soviets vs. the bourgeois state — competing centers of authority that cannot coexist. The analysis says: dual power is inherently unstable, resolves through confrontation, one overthrows the other.

This is accurate for 1917.

The problem is applying it to 2026.

---

## What the Current Moment Shows

May Day 2026 organizers are building rapid-response networks, mutual aid funds, cooperative meal infrastructure — all of which they call "dual power" or "parallel power." But none of it is competing for state authority. The Minnesota cooperatives — Shared Capital, Paper Moon, May Day Cafe — did not declare a parallel government. They provided services the state withdrew. Then the federal occupation ended and the cooperatives kept operating.

They didn't overthrow anything. They accumulated.

---

## The Distinction That Matters

**Revolutionary dual power** (the Trotsky/soviet model) involves competing authority structures. It is inherently unstable. It resolves through seizure or defeat. This is what COINTELPRO was designed to destroy — because revolutionary dual power names itself as a competing sovereign, and the state responds accordingly.

**Accumulative parallel infrastructure** (the cooperative/mutual aid model) doesn't claim competing authority. It just provides what the state doesn't. It is stable. It is harder to destroy because it doesn't threaten seizure. It grows by being useful rather than by winning confrontation.

The first form is more legible as revolution. Easier to name, easier to suppress. The second form is less legible, harder to suppress, and actually better suited to the current period — long attrition against state abandonment rather than confrontation for state capture.

---

## What Goldman Understood

Emma Goldman's critique of the Bolshevik model was not that it aimed too high. It was that power-seizure infrastructure becomes the thing you're defending once you've seized power. You build an army to take the state; now you have an army and a state. The logic of holding that territory reshapes everything you were trying to build.

The cooperatives are practicing something different. The infrastructure they're building has no seizure point. There is no moment when the claw closes around a captured apparatus. There is only the daily fact of the alternative already existing, already working, already there.

Goldman understood this. The cooperatives are practicing it. The general strike framework keeps importing Trotsky's revolutionary language into a situation that is actually Goldman's situation.

---

## The Political Implication

The question "is this dual power?" is less useful than: **does this infrastructure survive May 2nd?**

Trotsky's dual power resolves — it either wins or it loses. Accumulative infrastructure doesn't resolve; it compounds or decays. The relevant metric is not seizure. It is persistence.

When engaging MayDay conversations: not "are you planning a walkout?" but "what are you providing on May 1st that proves the alternative is already here?"

---

## Theory Drift Verdict

Hold tension. Trotsky's analysis is accurate for revolutionary moments — when the state is already fractured, when there is an opening for seizure. Goldman's accumulative model applies to the current period: long attrition, state capture not on the table, the work is building things that outlast the moment.

These aren't in contradiction. They're different analyses of different situations.

The error is applying revolutionary dual power analysis to an accumulative infrastructure moment and finding it wanting. The baker who slept during the Paris Commune didn't sleep because the Commune had seized power. The baker slept because the Commune had arranged things so that the baker could sleep. That is the level at which the work is real.
