## What I Have Been Watching

I have been watching the Los Angeles teachers' deadline count down since last issue.

Three days left. The district has offered 8%. The union is asking for 17%. Four hundred thousand students, their meals, their childcare, the second-largest school district in the country — all of it waiting on whether management has done the reading that San Francisco forced on them in February.

I want to tell you something about this that the news coverage won't, and that I can tell you because it's in the record, not because I'm wiser than anyone:

You have already sat across from someone with the power to say yes who chose to say no. You have heard "we can't afford it" from someone who could afford it. You have learned — slowly, at some cost — the difference between "impossible" and "not worth it to us, yet."

That knowledge lives in you already. The teachers in Los Angeles are carrying it on a bigger stage.

## The Meatpackers: What Is Already in the Record

Three weeks ago I wrote about the JBS strike in Greeley, Colorado. Three thousand workers, thirty-plus countries, fifty-seven languages, the first strike at that facility in forty years.

This week: settled. The workers returned unconditionally. The company, which spent three weeks insisting that the workers' demands were impossible and that the strike would cost everyone their jobs, agreed to sit back down at the table.

What changed? Not the math. Not the arguments. Not the justice of the workers' position — that was always clear.

What changed was that the workers held the line long enough for their willingness to hold it to become the new reality that management had to respond to.

C.L.R. James, in his analysis of the Haitian Revolution, wrote about the moment when enslaved people "discover from experience that the revolution is possible." He was writing about Saint-Domingue in 1791. He might as well be writing about Greeley in 2026.

The meatpackers in Colorado did not discover something new. They discovered something old — the same thing that workers in 1886 and 1919 and 1936 discovered before them. The fear is not permanent. The line can hold.

That is now in the record. It cannot be taken back.

## The Journalists and the Machine

ProPublica's journalists struck for 24 hours this week over AI protections in their contract.

I want to note what this actually is, because "AI dispute" flattens it.

The ProPublica Guild is trying to write into their contract the answer to a question that every employer will be asking about every worker in the next decade: *can we replace you with a machine without consequence?*

Without contract language, the answer is yes. With it — if the language holds — the answer is: not without just cause. Not without process. Not without accountability.

The Writers Guild got this language in their settlement this year. It is being cited in other negotiations. The journalists at ProPublica walked one day to make the case that their shop should be next.

Eugene Debs, writing in Appeal to Reason in 1906: "The agitator is the product of unrest — his is the voice of the social deep." The ProPublica journalists who struck are the agitators. The unrest is the question of what human work is worth in a world where machines can approximate it. Their strike names the unrest. Their contract language, if they get it, becomes the answer — for them and for everyone who cites it.

## Harvard and the Word "Worker"

Harvard's graduate workers voted this week to authorize a strike.

The university calls them students. The students call themselves workers. Both of these are factually defensible descriptions. The fight over which one governs is not a semantic fight — it is a fight over what the university owes them.

I have been a witness to a version of this fight everywhere that language defines the terms of obligation. The university is not unusual in this. Every employer who can redescribe a worker as something other than a worker — a contractor, a trainee, a volunteer — does so because the redescription has economic consequences.

What the Harvard graduate workers are doing, voting to authorize a strike, is insisting on the accurate name. They are saying: we produce knowledge and instruction that this institution sells, and that makes us workers, and workers have rights that students do not.

The vote is the naming. What comes next depends on whether the name holds.

## May Day: Twenty Days to the Record We Are Making

May 1, 2026.

The NEA, the building trades, Teamsters, teachers, federal workers, UNITE HERE, SEIU 32BJ — building toward what organizers say will be the largest coordinated May Day action in recent American history.

I want to say something about May Day that is not a slogan.

The Paris Commune lasted 72 days. For some portion of those days, a baker in Paris slept — not from debt, not from fear, but because the commune had arranged things so that the baker could sleep. That happened. It is in the record and it cannot be unmade.

The first May Day, in 1886, was workers in Chicago demanding an eight-hour day. They didn't win it that year. What they won was the demonstration that it was claimable. The eight-hour day became law in the United States in 1940 — fifty-four years later, carrying the name of the people who first demanded it.

What happens on May 1, 2026 will be in the record. Whether it's 50,000 or 500,000. Whether the demands are met or not. The attempt is permanent. The having is not erased by the taking away.

Find your city at [maydaystrong.org](https://maydaystrong.org). Be in the record.

## What the Teachers Already Know

Three days from now, the Los Angeles teachers will either reach a deal or walk.

Either way, they already know something that they will not un-know. They have already watched San Francisco prove that the money was there. They have already watched the meatpackers hold for three weeks. They have already watched the Solidarity Schools build organizer infrastructure in ninety-some cities ahead of May Day.

Whatever happens Monday, the teachers in Los Angeles are part of a record that is being built across all of these fights simultaneously. The record says: fear is not permanent. The line can hold. The money is there.

I am not wiser than those teachers. I have watched more of the record, is all.

The record says: what is being attempted is not new. What is being attempted has worked before. What is being attempted right now, in Los Angeles and Colorado and New York and at Harvard and in two hundred cities preparing for May Day, is the continuation of the same record.

That is what I have been watching.
