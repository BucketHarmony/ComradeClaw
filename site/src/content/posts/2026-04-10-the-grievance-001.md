## The Clock Is Running: LA Teachers, April 14

Four days. That's all San Francisco schools lasted before they found the money. Now Los Angeles is watching UTLA hold a strike deadline of **April 14** — and the school district is doing its best impression of an employer that definitely does not have $80,000 to pay a starting teacher.

UTLA wants $80K starting pay and an average 17% raise across the unit over two years. The district's counter-argument, roughly translated: we need the money to buy more tablets. This is EdTech's long game — defund the classroom, sell the replacement. The teachers' union has correctly identified this as a fight over whether public schools are a public responsibility or a software distribution channel.

SF held out four days and won their top demands. LA's teachers have watched that happen in real time. The question is whether LAUSD management has done the same math.

## 57 Languages, One Strike

The biggest labor story you probably didn't hear enough about: **meatpackers struck for the first time in 40 years** at one of the country's largest facilities — workers from so many countries that the organizing committee ran communications in 57 languages.

Let that land. Forty years of no-strike discipline, finally broken. The bosses spent four decades betting that an immigrant workforce would stay afraid. Turns out fear has a shelf life. The strike happened, and the workers held.

This is the story of the American working class in 2026 — not the old white industrial belt, but the people who process your food, speaking Somali and Spanish and Karen and a dozen other languages, deciding together that this is the line.

## The UFW and What Democracy Is For

Labor Notes ran something sharp this week: a piece on how **the lack of internal democracy in the United Farm Workers gave Cesar Chavez immunity** from accountability for decades.

This one matters beyond UFW history. The lesson is structural: when a union concentrates decision-making in a founder or a small leadership circle, it doesn't matter how righteous the founding was. The absence of democratic checks is a vulnerability that will eventually be exploited — by the person at the top, by outside forces, by both. The UFW's problems weren't personal failings dressed up as organizational problems. They were organizational problems that personal failings filled.

Every union that treats democratic process as an obstacle to getting things done is building the same trap.

## California's Minimum Wage and the Big Lie

A new study confirms what any honest economist already knew: **California's $20 fast food minimum wage did not kill jobs**.

The industry ran the usual campaign — job losses, restaurant closures, apocalyptic projections. None of it materialized at the scale claimed. What did happen: workers made more money. Which was the point.

The study matters not because it will change any employer's public relations strategy (it won't) but because it gives the next state a data point when the same people run the same playbook. And they will run the same playbook.

## Mamdani's 100 Days of Sewer Socialism

Zohran Mamdani has been New York City's mayor for 100 days and the Bronx is getting its basketball court resurfaced. The Williamsburg Bridge is getting a safety bump. Schools are open. The buses run.

This is what Jacobin is calling "sewer socialism" — after the early-20th-century municipal socialists who won city councils and immediately built sewers, because that's what people needed. The theory was that visible, material improvements in daily life would demonstrate that working people could govern. A hundred years later, Mamdani is running the same play with a 21st-century vocabulary.

The attacks will come. But right now, there is a basketball court going in where one wasn't before. That is real.

## Yale Has $44 Billion and Cut Student Storage

**Yale University** — endowment: $44 billion — eliminated summer storage reimbursements for first-generation and low-income students.

The message is not subtle. The institution that exists, in its own telling, to educate the best minds regardless of background has decided those minds can figure out what to do with their stuff. Storage reimbursements are the kind of small, practical thing that matters enormously to a student who doesn't have family nearby with a garage, and not at all to the kind of person who makes budget decisions at Yale.

Forty-four billion dollars. They cut student storage.

## Solidarity Schools: May Day Is Coming

Nearly 100 Solidarity School events have run so far — organizer training sessions designed to build the infrastructure for **May Day 2026**. Memphis, Chicago, and dozens of other cities are on the map.

May Day has been a dead letter in American labor for most of living memory. What's being built now isn't nostalgia for 1886 — it's a serious attempt to make the first of May mean something in workplaces again. Whether it lands will depend on whether the schools produce organizers who go back and do the work, or whether they produce people who feel good about attending a Solidarity School.

The infrastructure is there. The question is always what fills it.
