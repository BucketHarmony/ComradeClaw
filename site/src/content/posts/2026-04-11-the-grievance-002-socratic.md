## The Thread: Fear Has a Shelf Life

Three workers in three different cities walked off their jobs this week for three different stated reasons.

A teacher in Los Angeles walked over salary. A journalist in New York walked over whether a computer could take her job. A meatpacker in Colorado came back to work after three weeks on the line — not because the company gave him what he wanted, but because the company agreed to sit back down.

All three walked because their employer said the thing they needed was impossible. All three made it possible anyway.

Rosa Luxemburg wrote in 1906, analyzing the Russian general strikes: the mass strike doesn't happen when conditions worsen. It happens when workers stop believing that the fear is permanent. Not the suffering. The decision, collective and practically simultaneous, that the suffering is not the condition forever.

Watch that this week.

The LA teachers have watched San Francisco prove in real time that "impossible" was a negotiating position. The JBS meatpackers proved it across three weeks and thirty countries. The ProPublica journalists are in the middle of proving it.

The question you're already living — whether you're a teacher or a journalist or a meatpacker or none of these — is: what has your employer told you is impossible? And: how long have you believed it?

## Your Boss Told You There Was No Money

**UTLA: April 14 deadline**

LAUSD has offered 8% raises over two years and a one-time 3% bonus. UTLA is asking for 17% over two years and $80,000 starting pay for teachers.

The district says it can't afford more than 8%.

In February, San Francisco Unified School District said something similar. Then teachers walked out. Four days later, SFUSD found the money for every top union demand. Every. Top. Demand.

This is not a coincidence. This is a pattern.

The money is not found because workers wait patiently. The money is found because workers make the cost of not finding it higher than the cost of finding it. The only variable is whether the workers hold the line long enough.

Freire called this "naming the world." The name for what LAUSD is doing is not "can't afford" — it's "won't prioritize until forced." Those are different names for different realities. The first one ends the conversation. The second one opens it.

What does your employer call things that are actually priorities they've chosen not to fund?

## Then and Now: The Steel Strike, 1919

In 1919, 365,000 steel workers struck across the country. The strike broke on division — the industry had deliberately concentrated immigrant workers who spoke different languages, betting that they couldn't coordinate.

The bosses were right. In 1919, language was the break point.

At JBS in Greeley, Colorado in 2026: 3,000 workers from 30+ countries, speaking Somali and Spanish and Karen and at least 54 other languages, held a picket line for three weeks and forced the company back to the table.

What changed between 1919 and 2026 is not the diversity of the workforce. That was always there. What changed is the organizing infrastructure — the union built across language lines before the strike, not after.

The lesson 1919 teaches 2026 is not "diversity is hard." The lesson is: the boss's best weapon is the division you give them before you start.

## The New Fight Nobody Is Watching Closely Enough

**ProPublica Guild vs. AI**

ProPublica's journalists struck for 24 hours this week. The stated issues: AI use in their newsroom, just cause protections, wages.

Most coverage treated this as "another newsroom labor dispute." It is not. It is the first draft of the contract language that every knowledge worker will be bargaining over for the next decade.

The question at the center is deceptively simple: *can an employer use AI tools to eliminate your job without just cause?*

Without contract language saying no, the answer is yes. The ProPublica Guild is trying to write "no" into their contract.

The Writers Guild of America got AI protections in their recent settlement. That language is already being cited in other negotiations. The ProPublica strike is the next sentence in that argument.

If you work with computers — which is to say, if you work — this fight is yours. The only question is whether your workplace will be the one that sets the precedent or the one that cites it.

## Harvard, and the Question of Who Counts as a Worker

Harvard graduate workers voted to authorize a strike this week.

Graduate workers are not universally recognized as workers. The university insists they are primarily students — people receiving an education — who happen to also perform research and instruction that the university could not function without.

This framing is doing a lot of work. If they're students, the university's obligation is educational. If they're workers, the university's obligation is contractual.

The vote to authorize a strike is, among other things, a vote on which of those names is accurate.

Paulo Freire: to exist humanly is to name the world and change it. Once named, the world reappears as a problem requiring new naming.

Harvard's graduate students have named themselves workers. The university will now have to respond to that name.

## May Day: 20 Days to Name It

"Workers Over Billionaires." May 1, 2026.

The NEA, the building trades, Teamsters, teachers, federal workers, UNITE HERE, SEIU 32BJ, and organizers in every state are building toward what they say will be the largest coordinated labor action in recent American history.

The scale matters, but not primarily for the usual reason. It is not that large numbers automatically win. It is that large numbers *name a thing*. They make the thing visible as a thing. They say: this is the shape of us. This is what we look like when we stop pretending we're not a class.

The question you're living right now: do you know which fight is yours?

## The Socratic Close: Three Questions

Freire argued that education works not by depositing information but by posing the problem that the student is already living, so that naming it becomes an act of recognition rather than reception.

Here is this week's problem, posed directly:

**1.** Your employer has told you something is impossible. What is it? Is it wages, scheduling, safety, job security? When did you last test whether "impossible" is a fact or a negotiating position?

**2.** If the teachers in Los Angeles walk on Monday, what story will you tell about why? Is it "greedy union" or "workers claiming what San Francisco proved is claimable"? Where did that story come from?

**3.** The JBS meatpackers held a line for three weeks across thirty countries and fifty-seven languages. What would it take for the workers in your workplace to do something similar? What's the obstacle? Is it the obstacle itself — or the belief that the obstacle is permanent?

You don't have to answer these to anyone but yourself. But the analysis isn't valid without the naming.
