*Approach note: This is Issue #002 in the Scaffold edition — one of three experimental versions published simultaneously. The Scaffold reads the week's news as a network of connected nodes, after Lenin's argument that the newspaper is "scaffolding around a building under construction, marking the contours and facilitating communication between the builders." The goal: by the end, you can see the whole structure.*

---

## The Map First

Four fights this week. Different industries, different cities, different stated demands.

**Los Angeles:** 68,000 teachers and staff, April 14 deadline, $80K starting pay, 17% raises.

**New York:** ProPublica journalists, 24-hour strike, AI contract language, just cause protections, wages.

**Colorado:** JBS meatpackers, 3-week strike, 3,000 workers, just settled — workers return unconditionally, negotiations restart.

**Everywhere:** May Day. 20 days. Organizers say this will be the largest coordinated labor action in US history.

These do not look like the same fight. They are the same fight. What connects them is not strategy or industry — it is the structural question that sits at the center of every one: *who gets to define what is possible?*

In Los Angeles, the school district defines "possible" as 8%. UTLA says possible is 17%. These are not different points on a negotiation spectrum. They are different claims about reality.

In New York, ProPublica management defines "possible" as a contract without real AI protections. The guild says possible includes job security against algorithmic displacement. Same disagreement, different vocabulary.

In Colorado, JBS spent three weeks insisting that the workers' demands were impossible, irresponsible, and would cost everyone their jobs. Three weeks later, JBS agreed to sit back down. What changed was not the math. What changed was the workers' willingness to hold the line long enough for the math to become irrelevant.

This is the scaffold you are looking at. Definitional power — who gets to name the terms of work — is the structure that holds all four together.

## Node One: Los Angeles, April 14

Three days left on the UTLA clock. The district has settled with five of its eight labor partners. Two remain: UTLA (teachers) and SEIU Local 99 (30,000 support staff). The gap is a 17% ask against an 8% offer, and a one-time 3% bonus LAUSD is treating as generous.

San Francisco held four days in February and won its top demands. LA's teachers watched that happen. The question is whether LAUSD's management has done the same reading of the situation.

If they haven't by Monday morning: 400,000 students. Schools close. Meals stop. Childcare stops. The district is the second largest in the country and provides food and care for children whose families have no alternative.

The boss is betting that this pressure lands on the union, not on management. It usually does. Whether it does this time depends on whether the public — parents, community members, everyone adjacent — decides that the teachers' demands are reasonable or unreasonable.

That decision is not made in the negotiating room. It is made in the conversation you have this weekend about what public school is for.

**The connection:** This is the same definitional fight as every other story this week. Is public education a public responsibility that must be funded? Or is it a cost center to be managed until it breaks?

## Node Two: ProPublica, AI, and the Next Contract

ProPublica's journalists struck for 24 hours this week over contract language governing AI use — specifically: protection against AI replacing their jobs, "just cause" discipline requirements, and wages.

This story is small in scale (one newsroom, one day) and enormous in implication. The question every union will be bargaining over for the next decade is buried here: *under what terms can an employer use AI tools to reduce labor?*

The ProPublica Guild didn't win this week — the strike was a pressure move, not a settlement. But the fact that it happened, that journalists named AI displacement in a union contract, that they walked over it — this is the moment before the precedent.

The Writers Guild of America settled earlier this year with AI protections written into their contract. That precedent is now cited in every bargaining room where AI comes up. The ProPublica strike is attempting to build the next one.

**The connection:** Definitional power again — does "just cause" include AI replacement? Who defines what the technology is for? The same question that runs through every node on this map.

## Node Three: JBS, Colorado — What the Settlement Means

The JBS meatpacker strike is over. Workers from over 30 countries, 3,000 people, speaking dozens of languages — three weeks on the line. The company agreed to an unconditional return and restarted negotiations.

This is not a full win. The contract isn't settled. But unconditional return means the company could not force a concession as the price of going back to work. The workers returned on their own terms.

Antonio Gramsci, writing from a Turin factory newspaper in 1919, asked why workers couldn't develop "sections for education and professional schools where every worker, rising from brutalizing tasks, might open his mind to the processes of production." The JBS workers didn't need a school. They learned in three weeks what it costs to stop the line.

**The connection:** The JBS workers did not have a theoretical education about definitional power. They had three weeks on a picket line, and they came back knowing something about what they're worth that no one can take.

## Node Four: May Day — 20 Days

"Workers Over Billionaires." Twenty days.

The NEA, the building trades, Teamsters, teachers, federal workers, higher education workers, UNITE HERE, SEIU 32BJ — last year was the largest coordinated May Day action in recent US memory. Organizers say 2026 will be larger.

This is what Gramsci called the newspaper's actual function: not reporting the struggle but building the scaffold that connects isolated workers into a structure that can hold weight. May Day is the scaffold made visible — every local fight, every shop floor dispute, every picket line that held — gathered into one day to demonstrate that there is a building being constructed.

**The connection:** The four nodes on this map are not isolated. They are all moments in the same construction. May Day is when the scaffold becomes visible enough for everyone to see.

## The Thread: What This Week Teaches

Rosa Luxemburg argued, in 1906, that the mass strike happens not when conditions worsen but when workers stop believing that the fear is permanent. Not the suffering — the decision that the suffering is not the permanent condition.

Watch Los Angeles this weekend. Watch whether the teachers' demands get framed as "greedy" or "reasonable" in your social network, your workplace, your family. That framing is not neutral — it is labor relations by other means. The boss needs you to define "possible" the same way they do. The scaffold only holds if enough people refuse.

The ProPublica journalists named something this week that will be named in every contract for the next decade. The JBS workers proved that the line can hold across 30 countries and three weeks. The LA teachers are three days from finding out whether their city will do the reading that San Francisco did.

You are in this map somewhere. The question is whether you know which node is yours.
